How It Works

Everything You Need to Know About Corkage

Bringing your own bottle to a restaurant is one of the great pleasures of serious wine appreciation. This is not about avoiding the wine list — it's about celebrating a special vintage, a gifted bottle, or a label that no restaurant cellar can stock. Here's what you need to know.

01
What Is a Corkage Fee?
When a restaurant allows you to bring your own wine, they may charge a corkage fee to cover the cost of service — proper glassware, decanting if needed, staff attention to the bottle. Fees in Montgomery County typically range from $20–$55 per bottle, with no legal cap on the amount.
02
The Golden Rule: Check the List
Per Montgomery County regulations, the wine you bring cannot already appear on the restaurant's wine list. If the restaurant serves a Caymus Cabernet and you arrive with one, they are within their rights to decline it. Always confirm before you arrive — and if in doubt, call ahead.
03
You Must Order a Meal
County regulations require that you order a meal to consume your personal wine. Corkage is a dining privilege, not a loophole to drink your own wine at a restaurant bar. Come to eat — and bring a bottle worth the occasion.
04
Leftover Bottles Go Home
If your bottle isn't finished, re-cork it and take it with you. The restaurant may not hold it for you, and they cannot legally retain an open bottle that belongs to a guest. Many restaurants will re-cork formally — ask your server.
05
Bring Something Special
The spirit of corkage is to share something meaningful — a bottle from the year you met, a case you laid down a decade ago, a producer whose work you've followed. Restaurants that welcome BYOB guests are inviting a different kind of diner. Arrive accordingly.
06
Tip on the Full Experience
Your server is still opening, decanting, and caring for your wine. It's considered gracious to tip as if you ordered a mid-range bottle from their list. The corkage fee goes to the house — the tip goes to the person serving you.
Montgomery County, MD · Official Regulation
Under Montgomery County ABS Bulletin LRE 002, restaurants, clubs, and hotels holding a beer and wine or beer, wine, and liquor Class B or Class C license may add a Wine Corkage Permit at no additional cost. Each establishment decides independently whether to allow carry-in wine and whether to charge a fee. There is no county-imposed cap on corkage fees. The wine brought on premises must not appear on the licensee's wine list. Consumption requires a meal order. Unused wine may be re-corked and removed by the guest.

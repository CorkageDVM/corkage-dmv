The Smart Diner's Guide

Stack the Chase Sapphire Reserve $300 Dining Credit with BYOB

Bring your own bottle to skip the markup, and let the card cover up to $150 of the food — twice a year. Here's how the two fit together in DC.

The Chase Sapphire Reserve quietly became one of the better dining cards in 2025, with a credit worth up to $300 a year at a curated list of restaurants. Pair that with the oldest trick in fine dining — bringing your own wine — and a special dinner gets a lot lighter on the wallet. The catch is that this credit is pickier than most, so it pays to understand exactly how it works.

How the Sapphire Reserve Dining Credit Works

If you carry the Chase Sapphire Reserve, you're entitled to a dining credit worth up to $300 per year, split into two halves:

  • Up to $150 from January 1 through June 30, and another up to $150 from July 1 through December 31.
  • It applies automatically when you pay with your Sapphire Reserve at an eligible restaurant — no enrollment, and no need to book through any particular app.
  • Use it or lose it. The credit does not roll over; an unused first-half $150 is gone on July 1.
The Important Catch

This credit only works at restaurants in the Sapphire Reserve Exclusive Tables program — a curated list, powered by OpenTable, of upscale restaurants in major cities including Washington, DC. It is not "any restaurant." Before you count on it, open your Chase account (or the Sapphire Reserve Exclusive Tables site) and confirm the restaurant is on the current list — the lineup changes.

Why BYOB Makes It Even Better

The credit covers your check, but it does nothing about a marked-up wine list. That's where corkage comes in. At an Exclusive Tables restaurant that also allows BYOB, you get the best of both:

  • You bring a bottle you already own — skipping the restaurant's 200–300% markup.
  • You pay a corkage fee instead (in DC, often $35–$75 at this caliber of restaurant).
  • Your meal and corkage run through the Sapphire Reserve, working toward that $150 half-yearly credit.

The Play, Step by Step

  1. Confirm the restaurant is on Exclusive Tables. Check the current list in your Chase account — the credit only fires there.
  2. Check the corkage policy. Use the Corkage DMV directory to see whether that restaurant welcomes your bottle, and what it charges. Many of DC's Exclusive-Tables-caliber rooms book through OpenTable — the same upscale spots most likely to qualify.
  3. Bring the right bottle. Pick something that isn't on their list, and arrive prepared — see What to Bring When You BYOB.
  4. Pay with your Sapphire Reserve. The dining credit applies automatically; statement credits usually post within a billing cycle or two.
  5. Do it again after July 1. The second $150 unlocks in the back half of the year.
Two Cards, Same Trick

If you also carry an Amex Platinum, you can run the same play with its Resy credit on a different set of restaurants — see our Amex Platinum Resy credit guide. Between the two programs, a serious diner can offset a meaningful chunk of their dining out across the year, all while drinking their own wine.

A Few Honest Caveats

  • Order a meal — corkage is a dining privilege, not a way to drink at the bar.
  • Tip on the full experience — your server still opens and pours your wine; tip as if you'd ordered a comparable bottle.
  • Don't bring a wine on their list — the one near-universal corkage rule.
  • Confirm everything — both the Exclusive Tables list and corkage policies change; a quick call and a glance at your Chase account save surprises.
